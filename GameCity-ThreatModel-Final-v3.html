Executive Summary

Team 19 conducted a focused threat modeling exercise on the TMC Drive GameCity Online — Multiplayer Platform concept to identify high-impact threats, evaluate their business impact, and recommend actionable, cost-effective mitigations. Our results are constrained by multiple factors: limited experience in multiplayer game platform development, missing technical and security-relevant documentation, and absence of deeper financial risk simulations including applicable safety and compliance standards. Multiple assumptions were made and are labeled throughout this report.

This report presents a structured threat modeling assessment of the GameCity Online Multiplayer Platform, a browser-based city-building game ecosystem comprising 16 interconnected components spanning client SPA, API Gateway with WAF/rate-limiting/TLS termination, authentication (AgeConsent, MFA, password reset), user profiles, upload/media pipeline with virus scanning, game server cluster, real-time chat with DLP and parental controls, a virtual marketplace with purchase database, payment integration via external providers, CDN/asset hosting, databases, CI/CD infrastructure, background workers, moderation tools (flag review, ban/mute, auto-flag), async messaging (event bus, queue, DLQ), logging/SIEM, and secrets management.

The goal was to identify critical threat scenarios, their likelihood and business impact, financial exposure and operational consequences, and cost-effective mitigation strategies aligned with business growth. The assessment applies:

Key Findings

1. SQL/NoSQL Injection via API Gateway P0 CVSS-B 9.2
Unsanitized input forwarded through the API Gateway (past WAF/rate-limiting) to backend databases enables full database compromise. The architecture shows WAF and request validation at the gateway level, but ORM-level injection and NoSQL operator abuse bypass WAF pattern matching.
Impact: $4.2M-$12M data breach | DREAD 8.2 | PASTA Stage 7: Revenue loss + regulatory fines + litigation
2. Child Safety — Grooming via Chat P0 DREAD 8.6
Architecture shows AgeConsent in Auth Service and Parental Controls + DLP Flagging in Chat Service, but self-declared age verification is trivially bypassable. The "Underage Player" is explicitly modeled as an external actor, confirming the platform expects minor users. Behavioral grooming detection is absent.
Impact: $5M-$50M+ per incident | COPPA/DSA fines + lawsuits + platform delistment
3. RCE via Malicious File Upload P0 CVSS-B 9.1
Architecture shows a multi-stage upload pipeline (File Validation, Virus Scanner, Malware Scan, Metadata Extraction) before Object Storage, but polyglot files and parser-level exploits bypass signature-based scanning. If processing is not sandboxed, RCE enables lateral movement to internal services.
Impact: Full backend compromise | DREAD 7.8 | Server takeover + lateral movement to DB, secrets vault
4. Payment Webhook Spoofing P0 CVSS-B 8.8
Architecture shows external Payment Provider with webhook callbacks entering via the Route Entry point (Webhook path). Without cryptographic signature verification, attackers forge payment confirmations granting free entitlements at scale through the Marketplace Purchase DB.
Impact: $2.5M-$8M payment fraud | Revenue loss + chargeback fees + processor penalties
5. Race Condition Double-Spend in Marketplace P0 CVSS-B 7.7
The Marketplace (E-commerce, Catalog, Chain, Purchase DB, History) processes concurrent transactions. Non-atomic balance operations allow the same balance to be spent multiple times, creating virtual currency and destabilizing the in-game economy.
Impact: $1M-$5M economy damage | PASTA: Player churn + lost IAP revenue + brand damage
Bottom Line The platform presents a CRITICAL overall risk posture. Seven P0 vulnerabilities require immediate remediation within 0-14 days. Total estimated annual risk exposure of $16M-$86M+ justifies the recommended $1.1M-$2.2M security investment (7x-39x ROI). The child safety gap represents existential risk where a single incident could exceed the entire remediation budget by 10-50x.

Assessment Methodology

Frameworks, scoring models, and the transition from CVSS v3.1 to v4.0 for this assessment.

CVSS v4.0 — Why v4.0 Over v3.1

CVSS v4.0 was officially published by FIRST on November 1, 2023, and is the current industry standard. Key improvements over v3.1 that directly impact our scoring include: the new Attack Requirements (AT) metric capturing exploitation preconditions (critical for race conditions and timing attacks), replacement of the ambiguous Scope metric with explicit Vulnerable System and Subsequent System impact metrics, and the Supplemental metric group for industry-specific context. CVSS v4.0 scores are not directly comparable to v3.1 scores — several of our vulnerabilities score differently under v4.0 due to the improved granularity.

PASTA Business Impact Analysis

Process for Attack Simulation and Threat Analysis — 7-stage business-centric methodology mapping threats to business objectives, attacker capabilities, and financial outcomes.

Stage 1 — Define Business Objectives
Business Context and Objectives
GameCity Online is a browser-based city-building multiplayer platform targeting a broad audience including minors. Core business objectives: (1) Revenue generation through in-app purchases and virtual marketplace transactions, (2) User growth and retention through social features and competitive gameplay, (3) Regulatory compliance across COPPA, GDPR, PCI DSS, and DSA, (4) Platform trust and brand reputation, (5) Virtual economy stability as a core engagement driver.
Stage 2 — Define Technical Scope
Architecture-Informed Technical Scope
Based on the architecture diagram: 16 components spanning External Actors (Payment Provider, Developer, Underage Player, Bot, Player, Scripter, Moderator, Admin), Untrusted Zone (Web Client SPA with Offline Cache, File Upload, Browser Storage, JWT/Session, WebSocket, Service Workers, CAPTCHA), API Gateway (WAF, Rate Limiting, TLS Termination, Request Validation, Header Sanitization, Correlation ID), and Internal Services (Auth with AgeConsent/MFA, Upload/Media with multi-stage scanning, Chat with DLP/Parental Controls, Marketplace, Game Server, Profile, Workers, Moderation Tools, Logging/SIEM, Secrets Vault, Async Messaging with Event Bus/Queue/DLQ). External trust boundaries include Third-Party Integrations (Mod CDN/Server, Payment Provider) and CDN Asset Hosting.
Stage 3 — Application Decomposition
Data Flow and Trust Boundary Analysis
Critical data flows identified: (DF-01) Player Browser to API Gateway via HTTPS — crosses external/internal trust boundary, (DF-02) API Gateway to Auth Service — identity verification flow, (DF-03) API Gateway to Upload/Media pipeline — untrusted file processing chain, (DF-04) Chat Message Broker to other Players — user-generated content crossing user-to-user boundary, (DF-05) Payment Provider webhook to Marketplace Purchase DB — external third-party to internal financial data, (DF-06) CI/CD Pipeline to Production Deployment — supply chain trust boundary, (DF-07) Game Server to Player via WebSocket — real-time state synchronization.
Stage 4 — Threat Analysis
Attacker Profiling and Motivation
Five attacker profiles modeled from the architecture's External Actors: (A) Script Kiddie / Bot — automated attacks against public endpoints, credential stuffing, game cheating. Capability: Low-Medium. (B) Fraudster — payment webhook spoofing, refund abuse, double-spend exploitation. Capability: Medium. (C) Predator — targeting minors via chat, exploiting weak age verification. Capability: Low (social engineering). (D) Insider/Developer — access to CI/CD, secrets, admin tools. Capability: High. (E) Sophisticated Attacker — supply chain compromise, targeted RCE exploitation. Capability: High.
Stage 5 — Vulnerability and Weakness Analysis
Mapped Weaknesses per Data Flow
354 attack vectors identified and mapped to specific data flows and STRIDE categories. Top weaknesses: DF-01 carries injection and auth bypass risks (52 vectors), DF-03 carries RCE and malware upload risks (26 vectors), DF-04 carries XSS, grooming, and SSRF risks (25 vectors), DF-05 carries webhook spoofing and payment fraud risks (19 vectors), DF-06 carries supply chain and credential theft risks (13 vectors).

Stage 7 — Risk and Impact Analysis
Countermeasure Recommendations and Residual Risk
The architecture already incorporates several security controls (WAF, rate limiting, TLS termination, request validation at API Gateway; virus scanning and malware scan in upload pipeline; DLP flagging and parental controls in chat; AgeConsent and MFA in auth; SIEM and audit logs in monitoring). However, critical gaps remain: WAF does not prevent ORM-level injection, virus scanning misses polyglot exploits, AgeConsent relies on self-declaration, and webhook signature verification status is unknown. The residual risk after accounting for existing controls is detailed in the Residual Risk section. Total annualized risk exposure: $16M-$86M+.
